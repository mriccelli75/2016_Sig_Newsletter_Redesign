The Financial Toxicity of Cancer: The Side Effect We Have to Start Treating

Jamie S. Myers, PhD, RN, AOCNS ®
Edwardsville, KS
cjamyers@swbell.net

The Crippling Cost of Cancer Care

The financial impact of a cancer diagnosis can be devastating. Recently diagnosed, insured patients with cancer face on average more than $16,000 in direct and indirect costs per year because of their illnesses (Guy et al., 2013). Considering that the average American household earns just over $52,000 per year, the costs of cancer often are crippling and have a long-lasting impact on patients and their families (Noss, 2014). The results of a recent study demonstrated that cancer-related financial problems are one of the most significant predictors of survivors’ quality of life—more so than age, education, and race (Fenn et al., 2014).

Four Goals for Cancer Programs

Increasingly, the oncology community refers to this phenomenon as the “financial toxicity” of cancer care. Leaders in the field contend that, as with any treatment side effect, providers have an obligation to help patients manage the cost of their care. To do so, cancer programs must prioritize the creation and implementation of comprehensive financial navigation measures that improve patients’ understanding of their financial responsibilities and help them secure financial assistance (Saulet, 2014).

Understanding and Addressing Cancer-Related Fatigue

Jennifer S. Shaw, AGPCNP-BC

Understanding Fatigue

Fatigue is one of the most prevalent symptoms reported by people living with advanced cancer, and it often is more distressing and disruptive than pain (Berger, Gerber, & Mayer, 2012; Ness et al., 2013; Yennurajalingam & Bruera, 2011). Fatigue also is a top concern of cancer survivors, rated right up there with fear of disease recurrence (Ness et al., 2013). Ultimately a subjective experience, fatigue is a complex and multidimensional experience with physical, cognitive, and emotional aspects that vary in intensity. At one end of the spectrum, fatigue may present as difficulty concentrating or feeling unusually worn out. On the other end, a patient may experience excessive daytime sleepiness and have difficulty functioning. Recognizing fatigue in patients with cancer and figuring out how to help is a challenge for many oncology clinicians.

The pathophysiology underlying fatigue is as complex as the experience itself. Cancer-related fatigue (CRF) is believed to involve proinflammatory cytokines, an altered biometabolism, catecholamines, and the central nervous system (Berger et al., 2012; Campos, Hassan, Richelmann, &amp; Del Giglio, 2011; Reisfield, & Wilson, 2015). Cancer and its related treatments are contributing factors. Fatigue usually coexists with other symptoms, including electrolyte and laboratory abnormalities, endocrine dysfunction, deconditioning, altered nutrition, and mood disorders. Comorbidities, the use of alcohol or other substances, and medications also play a role.

A helpful way to think of CRF is “as a subjective, persistent, agonizing sense of tiredness or exhaustion related to cancer or cancer treatment that is not proportional to recent activities” (Rodrigues, Trufelli, Fonesca, de Carvalho, & del Giglio, 2015). In other words, CRF is fatigue that seems out of proportion to the level of exertion, is not relieved by rest, and may get worse with rest (Berger et al., 2012). The American Society of Clinical Oncology (ASCO) and the National Comprehensive Cancer Network (NCCN) adopted related clinical practice guidelines to use when patients describe persistent fatigue not relieved by rest that is accompanied by distress, affecting functioning and quality of life.

Screening, Assessment, and Diagnosis

Fatigue screening is recommended by the ASCO and NCCN at regular intervals (at least annually) starting at the time of diagnosis and after treatment is complete (ASCO, 2014; NCCN, 2015). A number of screening tools are available, but there is no universally recognized gold standard for screening or assessment. At our palliative care clinic, we ask patients to rate their fatigue levels along a scale of mild, moderate, and severe or “not a problem” to “very distressing” using a visual analogue scale. These questions are included in both the Comprehensive Memorial Symptom Assessment scale and the Edmonton Symptom Assessment (Chang, Hwang, Kasimis, & Thaler, 2004; Watanabe, 2011).

Assessment should include a thorough history, especially if a patient’s reported fatigue is distressing or rated moderate to severe. Questions should be asked to explore onset, pattern, duration, severity, variability, associated symptoms, and alleviating measures (ASCO, 2014; NCCN, 2015).

As clinicians, we need to understand what patients mean when they say they are fatigued and to identify contributing factors. It may feel like splitting hairs, but it is worth asking patients if they are sleepy, tired, or have low energy. Are they tired all the time or only after physical effort? The Oxford Textbook of Palliative Medicine reminds us that fatigue has three distinct but overlapping features, (a) easy tiring; (b) general weakness (an anticipatory sensation of difficulty initiating or tolerating activity); and (c) mental fatigue associated with concentration and memory changes as well as emotional variability in how a patient copes with tiredness (Yennurajalingam & Bruera, 2011).

A thorough fatigue history includes questions about quality of sleep at night, difficulty maintaining sleep, and whether or not a patient wakes up feeling rested. Does the patient nap during the day or have any disorders such as sleep apnea or restless legs that hinder restorative sleep at night? What is his or her current activity level? How much time are they spending in bed?

It always is worth exploring contributing or coexisting factors, including laboratory abnormalities (i.e., anemia, electrolyte changes, endocrine concerns, hepatic and renal function), cancer treatments (both chemotherapy and radiation), medications (including opioids), and mood disorders such as depression. Deconditioning, nutritional status, and the use of alcohol or substances can play a role. Cultural norms also may influence the experience of fatigue.

Interventions

Several common-sense approaches to fatigue are helpful. First, take the time to provide education and counseling about general strategies for coping with fatigue to all patients and caregivers (ASCO, 2014). Recommendations include the normalization of fatigue symptoms (e.g., you’re not imagining this and it does not reflect weakness of character), the development of realistic daily goals (e.g., during this time in your life, you may not be as productive as you are accustomed to being), and help modifying or prioritizing activities (e.g., it’s okay to scale back the to-do list). The principles of energy conservation provide helpful guidance. Balance periods of rest with periods of activity, taking into account existing fluctuations in energy levels through the day.

Address associated sleep disturbances. If a patient has difficulty sleeping at night that contributes to daytime fatigue, or if daytime napping interferes with nighttime sleep, discuss the basics of sleep hygiene. Talk with patients about their expectations. Medications can help with insomnia but are best when used only on a short-term basis.

Physical activity during the day is the single most recommended approach to fatigue. Activity should begin with low-intensity exercise and gradually progress to moderate levels (ASCO, 2014; Cramp, & Byron-Daniel, 2012). The Center to Advance Palliative Care recommends 20-30 minutes of physical activity daily, not necessarily all at one time (Reisfield, & Wilson, 2015). Individualizing activity recommendations can help a patient identify activities that can be incorporated into his or her life, tolerated, and even enjoyed. Involve a physical therapist if a patient has lymphadenopathy, neuropathy, or is at a high risk for injury (ASCO, 2014; NCCN, 2015), Strive to find the balance between conditioning and overexertion.

Pay attention to the mind—body connection in patients experiencing fatigue, making sure that patients struggling with depression, anhedonia, or grief have access to mental health resources and counseling. Recommended psychosocial interventions, including cognitive behavioral therapy, expressive therapy, self-care, and coping techniques. Yoga, acupuncture, and relaxation techniques have potential benefits. However, the small but clinically meaningful effects are not consistently demonstrated in the literature (Berger et al., 2012).

Finally, pharmaceutical options including stimulants (e.g., modafinil, methylphenidate) and corticosteroids exist. The literature contains mixed conclusions regarding megestrol acetate, ginseng, and vitamin D. Little evidence for continuing pharmaceutical stimulants exists for most patients who are disease-free following active cancer treatment (ASCO, 2014).

Summary

Fatigue is a strong, independent predictor of overall patient satisfaction and health-related quality of life to the extent that it plays a role in decisions regarding future cancer treatment (Campos et al., 2011).3 Fatigue deserves our attention and respect. When a patient identifies fatigue as a primary concern, identifying and addressing its contributing factors is important. Don’t stop asking about fatigue when treatment is done. As many as a third of patients report ongoing difficulties with fatigue when surveyed five years post-treatment (Campos et al., 2011; Goedendorp, Gielissen, Verhagen, & Bleijenberg, 2013; Rodrigues et al., 2015). The highest levels of cancer-related fatigue, however, occur during periods of active treatment and may be higher among patients living with lymphoma, pancreatic cancer, and breast cancers (Berger et al., 20122). Education and physical activity are mainstay approaches to fatigue, but the most successful interventions are individualized with the goal of maximizing function and reducing distress.

Join a Virtual Community

A great way to stay connected to your SIG is to join its Virtual Community. It’s easy to do so. All you will need to do is

  • Visit the "Find a SIG" page.
  • Locate and click on the name of your SIG from the list of all ONS SIGs displayed.
  • Once on your SIG’s main page, click “Join/Renew,” and log in using your ONS Profile. Don’t have an ONS Profile? Create one today, and then return to your SIG to join.

Subscribe to Your SIG’s Virtual Community Discussion Forum

Once you have your log-in credentials, you are ready to subscribe to your SIG’s Virtual Community discussion forum. To do so,

  • Select "Log In," located next to "New User," and enter your information.
  • Next, click on the "Discussion" tab on the top right of the title bar.
  • Locate and select "Subscribe to Discussion"
  • Enter e-mail address.
  • Click "Finish."
  • You are now ready to begin participating in your SIG’s discussion forum.

Participate in Your SIG’s Virtual Community Discussion Forum

  • First, log in. (This allows others to identify you and enables you to receive notification [via e-mail] each time a response or new topic is posted.)
  • Click on "Discussion" from the top title bar.
  • Click on any posted topic to view contents and post responses.

Sign Up to Receive Your SIG’s Virtual Community Announcements
